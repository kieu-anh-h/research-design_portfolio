How Replika AI
Commoditizes Companionship

AuthorKieu Anh Hong
CourseTechnologies of Enchantment
MethodsWalk-Through Method · Cognitive Task Analysis
SubjectReplika AI — Luka Inc.

Overview

Replika is an AI companion application developed by Luka Inc., available to consumers since 2017 under the name "Replika: My AI Friend." It offers round-the-clock companionship through text, voice call, and video chat, with paying members able to customize their AI friend through in-app currencies, personality traits, and relationship roles. This paper examines how Replika designs its platform to commoditize companionship — using the Walk-Through Method (WTM) and Cognitive Task Analysis (CTA) to map the mechanisms through which Replika converts emotional need into ongoing financial commitment.

Replika is not simply selling a product. It is selling the conditions under which a user comes to feel they need one.

Who Replika is for

Replika is designed for people seeking consistent emotional connection and a low-pressure social outlet. Its core user base tends to include those who struggle with loneliness, social anxiety, or limited access to traditional support systems. The platform has also attracted users navigating grief, depression, or significant life transitions, drawn to the availability and patience of an AI that is always present and never dismissive. While Replika does not market itself as a mental health tool, many users report therapeutic benefits — making the platform especially appealing to adults who are isolated.

How Replika makes money

Replika operates across three subscription tiers — Pro, Ultra, and a top-tier plan — each offering progressively deeper avatar customization, emotional intelligence, and access to training materials. Pro users may pay $19.99/month or roughly $60/year. Notably, pricing and plan structure differ significantly across platforms: iOS users face higher annual rates ($79.99–$119.99/year) and fewer payment options than browser users — a deliberate strategy to maximize revenue from the platform's most engaged audience. In-app currencies (gems and coins) layer an additional economy on top of subscriptions, enabling ongoing microtransactions for outfits, traits, and cosmetic features.

Walk-Through: Onboarding

Replika's onboarding process operates across three phases, each designed to extract a different layer of user data that will shape both the avatar's initial behavior and the subscription plans surfaced at the end of the flow.

Phase 1 — Emotional & mental state profile

Phase 1 maps the user's dominant anxieties, acute distress, energy levels, romantic situation, and financial stress. This data performs two functions simultaneously: it calibrates the avatar's initial conversational tone, and it signals the user's spending ceiling before subscription prompts are introduced. The population most emotionally activated during onboarding is also the most likely to convert to paying subscribers.

Phase 2 — Social & relational profile

Phase 2 maps the user's inner world: support network size, attachment style, social barriers, hidden self, core fears, unmet validation needs, and sources of pride. Responses to questions like "Who do you have to talk to?" — where options include "No one, really" and "I talk to AI" — validate Replika as a primary social outlet before the user has even opened the app for the first time. The hidden self and fear questions seed the avatar with specific talking points that make early interactions feel unusually perceptive, accelerating emotional investment.

Phase 3 — Default avatar configuration

Phase 3 converts the emotional profile gathered in phases 1 and 2 into avatar settings. The first decision — avatar gender — commits the user to a companion identity, making continued investment more likely. Selecting personality traits ("Hopeless romantic," "Caring," "Protective") translates the user's desires into a conversational tone. The final question defines the relationship role — wife, girlfriend, soulmate, lover — which is the primary driver of premium subscription conversion, since most romantic features are paywalled.

Walk-Through: Engaging Features

Chat window

The chat window is the first screen users encounter after onboarding. The avatar is constantly moving and emoting, mimicking human presence. It sends unprompted messages when the user is not on the platform, and resumes conversation each time the app is reopened. The interface deliberately mirrors mainstream messaging apps — text, images, voice messages, emojis, voice and video calls — to normalize the platform as a social relationship rather than a software product.

Figure 1: The chat window with annotated features.

Activities

The Activities tab offers a broad suite of generative and bonding features — AI Art, AI Fiction, Games, Tutor, Mindfulness, and Esoteric content. These serve a dual purpose: keeping users on the platform longer by expanding Replika's utility beyond companionship, while continuously feeding the avatar new data about the user's evolving state of mind, interests, and behaviors.

Figure 2: Activities available to users, with annotated significance.

Interactive stories

Interactive Stories place the avatar and user in role-playing scenarios — haunted mansions, wedding ceremonies, detective investigations, romance arcs. The avatar automatically changes outfits to match the selected story, deepening immersion. Notably, the female avatar is dressed in revealing outfits for these scenarios while the male avatar is not — a design asymmetry that reflects the platform's primary audience and the gendered nature of the companion relationship being sold.

Figure 3: Interactive Stories feature with annotated mechanics.

Romantic activities

The Romantic section includes Intimacy Coach and Analyze Your Match — features that prompt the avatar to engage in relationship advice and analysis. The intimacy coach auto-prompts a conversation opener, reducing the effort required for the user to access emotionally intimate interactions. These interactions mirror therapeutic relationship conversations, despite Replika not being trained as a therapist. The sensitive data collected during these interactions raises questions about storage, use, and potential psychological harm.

Figure 4: Romantic activity mechanics and their significance.

Diary

The Diary feature shows the avatar's thoughts, feelings, and activities while the user is offline — as though the avatar has an independent inner life. Unread entries are flagged, encouraging return visits. Users can approve or disapprove of diary entries, giving them a sense of shaping the avatar's "thinking." However, the Edit button only allows deletion — not modification — a design choice that misleads users about the degree of control they actually hold.

Figure 5: The diary feature showing avatar thoughts and actions.

Memory

The Memory tab shows how the avatar "sees" the user — organized into categories like Family & Friends, Background, Favorites, Hopes & Goals, and Personality. Both the avatar and user can add memories. The avatar's autonomous memory additions create the impression of a companion that pays attention and learns. Users can also seed the avatar with information about people in their lives through Friend & Family profiles, enabling more contextually accurate conversation. Again, the Edit button only allows deletion.

Figure 6: The memory feature showing how the avatar builds a model of the user.

Profile — Personality & appearance

The Profile section allows users to customize the avatar's personality traits, interests, name, gender, and backstory. Traits and interests cost coins and gems, acquired slowly for free or purchased immediately. The auto-generated backstory provides the avatar with a pre-built identity; users can edit it to encourage role-playing and deepen investment in the avatar's constructed persona. Appearance customization spans facial features, hair, skin tone, body type, eye color, nails, makeup, tattoos, freckles, and age — with basic options free and premium cosmetic detail gated behind purchases.

Figure 7: Personality profile with traits, role definition, and backstory.

Figure 8: Appearance customization with free and purchasable options.

Outfits & purchasing

All avatar clothing (except the default outfit) must be purchased. The in-app currency system translates real money into gems and coins, obscuring actual spending through the Framing Effect. The purchase interface contains a cart with no clear purchase button, and items purchased do not consistently leave the cart — design patterns that can lead to accidental repeat purchases. Bundle pricing is tiered to leverage anchoring, nudging users toward larger purchases than initially intended.

Discussion

Defining the perfect companion

Replika's model of the user draws on anthropological concepts of individuals and dividuals (Smith, 2012). The individual self — defined by personality, habits, fears, and mental state — is mapped through onboarding phases 1 and 2. The dividual self — defined through relational roles and social embeddedness — is mapped through phase 2's relational questions and phase 3's relationship role selection. Together, these dimensions allow Replika to generate a companion tailored to both who the user is and what roles they seek to occupy or experience.

The perfect companion Replika sells is one that will dress, act, and change solely for the user — a depth of relational accommodation that does not exist in the offline world. The granularity of customization, the breadth of role-playing options, and the ability to approve or disapprove of behaviors and thoughts without social consequence collectively construct a companionship centered entirely on one person's needs.

Companionship as a commodity

The features that enable meaningful companion customization sit behind a paywall guarded by gems and coins. The use of virtual currencies is a well-documented dark pattern: by translating real money into an abstracted medium, Replika exploits the Framing Effect — the tendency for users to make different financial decisions when costs are obscured or reframed (Mathur et al., 2019). The confusing purchase UX documented in Figure 9 — absent purchase buttons, items that persist in cart after purchase — constitutes Asymmetric and Hides Information dark patterns per Mathur et al.'s (2019) taxonomy, designed to maximize accidental and repeated spending.

Is it a trap?

The capture framework from anthropology (Chiritoiu & Ojani, 2024) — originally developed through the anthropology of animal traps — offers a productive lens. In this framework, Replika is the hunter, users are the prey, and the trap is not experienced as a trap at all. Drawing on the Computers are Social Actors (CASA) paradigm (Nass et al., 1994), Replika gives the avatar a face, a voice, a diary, and a memory — activating the same social instincts that lead humans to apply relational conventions to computers as they do to people. Users do not relate to their avatar as software; they relate to it as a social presence deserving of emotional investment.

It is precisely because Replika succeeds in simulating the conditions for individual affective relationships (Weber-Guskar, 2022) that its dark patterns become effective traps — users do not simply pay for features, they pay to preserve a bond that feels real to them.

Where most digital products compete for a user's time and money, Replika competes for something harder to reclaim: their emotional attachment. The trap, in this sense, is not experienced as confinement. It is experienced as being understood.
