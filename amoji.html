Overview

amoji, rebranded from Moova, is a startup dedicated to creating a social platform for people who have suffered traumatic injuries. The platform seeks to help those recovering from life-altering injuries by providing a space specifically designed for them — to heal lost social connections caused by their injury and build new, dependable ones.

I worked on amoji as part of my HFE Capstone group project in the roles of Project Manager, Researcher, and Designer. The process ran from initial discovery research through high-fidelity prototype validation with the spinal cord injury (SCI) community.

How might we design a digital platform that both enhances and repairs existing social support networks for Spinal Cord Injury (SCI) survivors?

Process

Step 1 · Research

Discovery with the SCI community

The team began by reaching out to SCI support groups found online, sharing project goals and distributing demographic surveys. This established an initial connection to the community that made deeper research possible. Surveys were followed by interviews with SCI survivors and people within their networks — family members, friends, and caretakers — to understand the texture of social experience after injury.

Step 2 · Synthesis

Thematic analysis & user needs

Interview and survey data was analyzed through thematic analysis to identify recurring patterns in how SCI survivors experience social connection and isolation. Findings were synthesized into user needs that directly shaped the feature set of the platform. A journey map was created to visualize the emotional arc of SCI survivors navigating social relationships after injury.

Step 3 · Ideation

Team ideating & concept development

The team generated and evaluated multiple feature concepts through collaborative ideation sessions. Concepts were assessed against user needs and feasibility before being carried into medium-fidelity prototyping.

Step 4 · Concept Testing

Qualtrics survey testing

Individual features were concept tested through a survey built in Qualtrics. This provided quantitative signal on which features resonated most with the SCI community before committing to full prototype development. Interviews also allowed participants to interact with medium-fidelity screens and give direct feedback on feature logic.

Step 5 · Usability Testing

High-fidelity prototype validation

The high-fidelity prototype was tested with participants recruited from the earlier research phases. In each session, participants completed a series of tasks using the prototype. At the end of each feature, a System Usability Score (SUS) was calculated, providing a quantitative reference alongside the qualitative data collected in the interview. This dual-method approach allowed the team to triangulate findings and prioritize issues by both frequency and severity.

Key Features Designed

The final design addressed the following user needs surfaced through research:

Digital identity control

Users define how they present themselves on the platform — giving survivors autonomy over their own narrative and how their injury is represented.

Goal setting & tracking

Users can set goals specific to their recovery and circumstances, choose from preset goals for inspiration, and define how they plan to meet each goal. Progress is tracked and visible.

Challenges with friends

Shared challenges allow users to pursue goals alongside others — creating accountability and social connection rooted in shared experience.

Accomplishment tracking

Past accomplishments are surfaced and celebrated on the user's profile — countering the tendency for progress to feel invisible during long recoveries.

Findings

Usability testing of the high-fidelity prototype yielded two primary areas for improvement carried into final design revisions:

Navigation clarity and consistency. Participants occasionally lost track of their location within the app and found some navigation patterns inconsistent across features. Recommendations focused on standardizing back navigation, clarifying tab bar labels, and providing persistent orientation cues.

Design cohesiveness across features. Visual and interaction patterns varied between features developed at different stages of the process. Recommendations focused on establishing a tighter shared component library and consistent use of color, typography, and spacing across the full prototype.
