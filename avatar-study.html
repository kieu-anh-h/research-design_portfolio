HCI Research · Study Design · 2025

Avatar Customization
& Avatar Identification

RolePrincipal Investigator
FrameworkProteus Effect · SDT
Planned N160 participants
SettingControlled lab study

Overview

This study was designed to investigate how the gender and species of a virtual avatar influences the type of identification a player forms with that avatar, and whether different identification types lead to meaningfully different in-game behaviors. The study is grounded in two connected bodies of literature: research on avatar identification and the Proteus Effect.

Proteus Effect

In mediated environments, users will behave in ways that their avatar's physical characteristics suggest — regardless of their own identity. The avatar acts as a behavioral cue.

Avatar Identification

The perception a user holds of their avatar in relation to themselves. Comes in three forms: similarity (the avatar reflects who I am), wishful (the avatar reflects who I want to be), and embodied (I experience the game through the avatar).

Avatar Customization

The act of modifying an avatar's physical characteristics — including gender expression and species — to align with self-perception or desired mediated identity.

Hypotheses

H0

The gender and species of an avatar has no effect on similarity, embodied, or wishful identification.

H1

There are no differences observed in the behaviors of users that have different identifications with their avatar.

Study Environment

Physical environment

The lab space is organized into computer and discussion rooms accommodating 40 participants simultaneously. Each participant has their own computer workstation. Observers are positioned separately. Food is provided to support participant comfort during sessions.

Virtual environment — activities

Once in the virtual space, participants engage in three activity types: one-on-one chat, group discussion, and a racing game. These were selected to probe different social and achievement-oriented behaviors that map onto identification types.

Customization options

Participants choose their avatar from three species categories, each with distinct sexual characteristics:

Human Bird — Crimson-backed Tanager Bird — Thick-billed Euphonia Bird — Green Honeycreeper Insect — Rhinoceros Beetle Insect — Luna Moth Insect — Honey Bee

Species were selected specifically to include options with sexually dimorphic physical characteristics, allowing the study to examine how gendered visual cues in non-human forms influence identification.

Behavioral tracking

The virtual environment tracks frequency of engaging in each activity, time spent per activity, chat logs, emotional state, and participant focus.

Participants

A target of 160 participants was determined to provide sufficient statistical power to detect a medium effect across the targeted variables. Participants are required to be over 18, reside within travelling distance to the lab, and be proficient with WASD movement and third-person avatar control.

Recruitment uses social media flyers followed by a screening survey to verify eligibility. No financial incentive is provided.

Procedure

1

Avatar customization (10 min). Participants customize their avatar using the controls provided. They select species, gender expression, and relevant visual attributes. What species and gender they choose is documented.

2

Virtual environment session (80 min). Participants enter the virtual environment and may chat one-on-one with other players, join group discussions, and play the racing game. Behavioral data is passively collected throughout — activity frequency, time on each activity, chat log, and group time.

3

Post-session survey (10 min). Participants complete the Player Identification Scale and the PANAS Affect Scale to measure identification type and emotional state. Results are used to classify each participant into an identification type.

4

Group interview (80 min). Participants are broken into small groups and asked about their motivations for customization, their perception of their avatar in relation to themselves, and their interest in controlling avatar gender and species. Responses feed into the thematic analysis.

Analysis Approach

For H0 — avatar characteristics and identification type

A Multinomial Logistic Regression is applied, using participant gender, avatar gender, and avatar species to predict avatar identification type (none, similarity, embodied, or wishful). This method is appropriate because the outcome variable has four categorical levels, and the predictors include both binary (gender) and multi-class (species) variables.

For H1 — identification type and behavior

A mixed qualitative and quantitative approach is used. Thematic analysis is applied to group interview transcripts, coding participants' statements about customization motivation, avatar perception, and desired control over gender and species. A One-way ANOVA is then used to determine whether identification type significantly predicts time spent across the three activities — with affect scores providing a reference for how engaging in different behaviors makes participants feel.

The design deliberately pairs quantitative behavioral tracking with qualitative interview data to avoid the limitation identified in type-based player frameworks — that similar behavioral outcomes can arise from fundamentally different motivations.
